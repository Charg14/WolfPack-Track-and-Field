NCAA Eligibility Process

To compete in NCAA Division I or II athletics, you must meet academic standards and register with the NCAA Eligibility Center.

Key Steps for NCAA Eligibility

  • Register with the NCAA Eligibility Center:

    Create an account at ncaaeligibilitycenter.org during your sophomore year of high school. Choose between a "Certification Account" (for D1/D2 athletes) or a "Profile Page" (for D3 or undecided athletes).

  • Meet Core Course Requirements:

    Complete a specific number of NCAA-approved core courses in subjects like English, Math, Science, Social Science, and additional English/Math/Science or World Language. The exact number and distribution vary by division.

    • Division I: 16 core courses. 10 of these must be completed before your senior year, and 7 of those 10 must be in English, Math, or Natural/Physical Science.
    • Division II: 16 core courses.
  • Achieve Required GPA in Core Courses:

    Maintain a minimum GPA in your NCAA-approved core courses. This GPA is calculated on a 4.0 scale.

    • Division I: Minimum 2.3 GPA.
    • Division II: Minimum 2.2 GPA.
  • Submit Test Scores (SAT/ACT):

    Send your official SAT or ACT scores directly to the NCAA Eligibility Center using code 9999. Division I uses a sliding scale combining GPA and test scores (higher GPA requires lower test score, and vice versa). Division II requires a minimum SAT sum score or ACT sum score.

  • Request Final Amateurism Certification:

    Once you commit to an NCAA school, the school will request your final amateurism certification. You'll need to answer questions about your sports participation, financial aid, and any professional sports involvement.

  • Send Official Transcripts:

    Ensure your high school counselor sends your official transcripts to the NCAA Eligibility Center after your junior year and again after graduation with proof of graduation.

Important Note: Always check the most current requirements on the official NCAA Eligibility Center website, as rules can change. Work closely with your high school counselor and coaches to ensure you are on track.

üçé Nutrition Plan for Athletes

Fueling your body properly is as crucial as your training. This plan provides guidelines for year-round nutrition, including in-season and pre-meet strategies.

Year-Round Principles

  • Balanced Macronutrients: Aim for 40-55% Carbohydrates, 25-35% Protein, and 15-30% Healthy Fats.
  • Hydration: Drink at least 1 gallon (approx. 4 liters) of water daily. Increase intake during and after workouts.
  • Consistent Fueling: Eat small meals or snacks every 2-3 hours to maintain energy levels and stable blood sugar. Never skip meals.
  • Whole Foods First: Prioritize fruits, vegetables, lean meats, whole grains, nuts, seeds, and low-fat dairy. Limit processed foods, excessive simple sugars, and unhealthy fats.
  • Post-Workout Recovery: Consume carbohydrates and protein within 30 minutes after exercise to replenish glycogen stores and aid muscle repair.
  • Listen to Your Body: Pay attention to how different foods affect your energy and digestion.

Off-Season Nutrition

Focus on building a strong foundation, muscle growth, and recovery. Carbohydrate needs may be slightly lower than in-season, but remain crucial.

  • Maintain a balanced diet with adequate protein for muscle repair and growth.
  • Continue consistent hydration.
  • Experiment with new healthy foods to expand your diet.
  • Ensure sufficient calorie intake to support training volume and recovery.

In-Season Nutrition

Focus shifts to fueling performance, rapid recovery between sessions/meets, and maintaining energy levels.

  • Higher Carbohydrate Intake: 5-7 grams of carbohydrates per kilogram of body weight per day (e.g., 375-525g for a 75kg/165lb athlete). This is your primary fuel.
  • Adequate Protein: 1.2-1.7 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day (e.g., 90-128g for a 75kg/165lb athlete), spread throughout the day.
  • Strategic Hydration: Carry a water bottle, drink consistently throughout the day, and use sports drinks for sessions longer than 1 hour or in hot/humid conditions.
  • Easy-to-Digest Foods: Especially around workouts and competitions, choose foods that you know your body tolerates well.

Pre-Meet Meals & Snacks

Proper fueling before a meet can significantly impact performance. Avoid trying new foods on meet day.

  • 3-4 Hours Before: A balanced meal high in complex carbohydrates, moderate in lean protein, and low in fat and fiber.
    • Examples: Pasta with light tomato sauce & lean ground turkey/chicken, grilled chicken with rice & steamed veggies, oatmeal with fruit & a small amount of nuts, turkey sandwich on whole-grain bread with fruit.
  • 1-2 Hours Before: Smaller, easily digestible snack, primarily carbohydrates.
    • Examples: Banana with a small amount of nut butter, oatmeal, bagel with jam, plain granola bar (low sugar), fruit smoothie.
  • 30-60 Minutes Before: Quick, simple carbohydrates.
    • Examples: Applesauce pouch, energy chews, handful of pretzels, fruit snacks, a few graham crackers.
  • Foods to Avoid: High-fat (fried foods, heavy sauces, greasy meats), high-fiber (beans, broccoli, large salads), and high-lactose foods (if sensitive) right before competition.

Healthy Snacks for Athletes

Keep these handy for sustained energy and quick recovery throughout the day, especially between events.

  • Carbohydrate & Protein Combos:
    • Greek yogurt with berries and granola
    • Peanut butter and jelly sandwich (on whole wheat)
    • Hard-boiled eggs with whole-grain crackers
    • Cottole cheese with fruit (e.g., pineapple)
    • Chocolate milk (excellent post-workout recovery)
  • Quick Carbs:
    • Bananas, apples, oranges, grapes, dried fruit
    • Pretzels
    • Plain bagels or rice cakes
  • Protein & Healthy Fats:
    • Nuts and seeds (almonds, walnuts, chia seeds)
    • Hummus with veggie sticks or whole-grain crackers
    • Edamame
    • Trail mix (nuts, seeds, dried fruit, minimal chocolate chips)
  • Portable Options: Granola bars (check sugar content), protein bars (whole food based), fruit pouches.
